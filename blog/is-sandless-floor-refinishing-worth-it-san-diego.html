Is Sandless Floor Refinishing Worth It?

Published 2026-07-21 by Top Tier Custom Floors

Is sandless floor refinishing worth it?

Sandless refinishing is worth it only when the existing finish is still intact and the damage is limited to surface scratches. A buffer abrades the top coat and a fresh coat goes over it, which hides light wear for two to four years. It cannot remove gray water stains, pet damage, or gouges that reach bare wood.

Last updated: July 2026

A woman off Cole Grade Road called us last spring because a company had quoted her $1.80 a square foot to make her floors look new in one day. No dust, no moving out. She wanted to know why we had quoted $4.25.

Her floors had gray stains under the kitchen window where a planter had leaked for years.

That job was never a sandless job. Gray means water got past the finish and oxidized the wood itself. No amount of buffing touches that, because buffing never reaches the wood.

What sandless refinishing actually does

The crew screens the floor with a 180 or 220 grit maroon pad on a rotary buffer. That scuffs the old polyurethane so a new coat sticks. Then one or two coats of water-based finish go down and cure overnight.

Everything happening there is above the wood. The old finish stays. The new finish bonds to it.

So the question is simple: is the damage in the finish, or in the wood?

Fingernail test

Drag a fingernail across a scratch. If it catches, the scratch is in the wood and screening will not remove it. If your nail glides over it, it is a finish scratch and a screen and coat will make it disappear.

When we recommend it

Rental turnovers. Houses going on the market in 30 days. Floors that were properly refinished four or five years ago and just look dull in the traffic lanes.

We screened and coated about 1,100 square feet in a Hidden Meadows house last February for $2,400. The floors were seven years old, no stains, just a satin finish worn to bare shine in front of the sink. It came out clean and the owners will get four good years before they need the real thing.

When it fails

The big one is wax. If anyone ever put a wax or an oil-based polish product on the floor, the new coat will not bond. It beads, it peels in sheets, and now you are paying for a full sand anyway on top of what you already spent.

We wipe a test patch with denatured alcohol before we quote. If the rag comes back with a waxy haze, we tell people straight that screening is off the table.

Aluminum oxide factory finishes on prefinished engineered plank are the other problem. That coating is hard enough that a maroon pad barely scuffs it, and bevel edges between boards trap the new finish in lines you can see across the room in afternoon light.

What we actually charge

Screen and coat runs $1.75 to $2.75 per square foot depending on how much furniture we move and whether there are stairs. Full sand and refinish is $3.75 to $5.50. Our sanders run on dust containment, so the difference in mess between the two is smaller than people expect.

Most San Diego County homes we look at with 15 year old oak need the full job. But not all of them. And we will tell you which one you have before you spend anything, because a screen and coat on a floor that needed sanding is money you spend twice.
